Does Buying Secondhand Actually Help the Planet?

5 min read · The Planet

The sustainability pitch for secondhand clothing gets thrown around a lot, often without much explanation. Here's what the actual UK data says — and where the claims get oversold.

The scale of the problem, in real numbers

Recent research from WRAP (the UK's Waste and Resources Action Programme) found that people in the UK throw away 49% of all used textiles straight into general waste rather than donating or recycling them, with the average person binning around 35 items of clothing every single year. Of the textiles that do end up in household waste, the large majority is incinerated rather than recycled, with a smaller share sent to landfill. On top of that, the UK buys more clothing per person than any other country in Europe, with consumption levels having climbed back to near pre-pandemic highs.

What buying secondhand genuinely does

  • Keeps existing items out of that 49%. Every secondhand purchase is one item that stayed in circulation instead of going straight into a bin destined for incineration or landfill.
  • Reduces demand for new production, even if only marginally. WRAP's own tracking of reuse and recycling schemes found that when recycled or reused items replace new purchases, it measurably cuts both carbon and water impact per tonne of textiles produced.
  • Extends the life of resources already spent. Every garment already carries the water, energy, and labour cost of having been made — keeping it in use longer spreads that original cost across more wear, rather than requiring it all over again for something new.

Where the claims get oversold

Buying one secondhand jacket won't offset a habit of otherwise buying large volumes of new fast fashion — the impact is real but incremental, not dramatic on an individual level. And secondhand shopping isn't a free pass to buy more than you actually need; the environmental benefit comes specifically from replacing new purchases, not from stacking extra consumption on top of them.

The honest version

The data backs the core idea: the UK has a genuine overconsumption and textile waste problem, and secondhand buying is one concrete way to be part of the solution rather than the problem. But it works best as one sensible habit among several — buying less overall, keeping items in use longer, and choosing secondhand where it makes sense — rather than a single purchase doing all the work on its own.
