Are used clothes actually worth it?

5 min read · Buying Guide

Everyone's got an opinion on secondhand clothing — either it's the obvious smart move, or it's "someone else's old stuff." The truth sits in the details, not the headline. Here's the honest breakdown.

The case for buying used

  • Older stock is often better made. A lot of clothing from 10-20 years ago used heavier fabrics and sturdier construction than fast-fashion equivalents made today. You're not always trading quality for a lower price — sometimes you're getting more of both.
  • You pay for the item, not the markup. New retail prices carry marketing, packaging, and retail margin. Secondhand strips all of that out.
  • One-off and discontinued pieces. Certain colourways, prints, and cuts simply aren't made anymore — buying used is sometimes the only way to get them at all.
  • Lower risk per item. If a £15 secondhand jacket doesn't work out, that's a much easier loss to absorb than a £70 new one.

The real downsides

  • Sizing isn't consistent. Brands change their fit over the years, and without trying it on, you're relying on measurements over labels.
  • Wear isn't always visible in photos. Pilling, thinning fabric, and small tears can be missed unless the seller photographs closely and describes honestly.
  • No easy returns. Unlike a shop, you can't just walk it back in five minutes — though platforms like Vinted do offer buyer protection if something's genuinely not as described.
  • It takes more time. Finding the right piece secondhand means more scrolling and more patience than grabbing something new off a shelf.

Where it's a clear win

Basics you'll wear hard — denim, leather, wool knitwear — tend to be the best secondhand buys, since older stock in these categories was often built to last longer in the first place. Trend pieces you'll only wear a season or two are also low-risk secondhand, since you're not overpaying for something with a short shelf life anyway.

Where to be more careful

Anything relying on precise fit — tailoring, structured outerwear — is worth checking measurements closely on. And always ask for extra photos of stress points (seams, cuffs, soles) if they're not already shown.
