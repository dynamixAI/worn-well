Why Secondhand Clothing Never Fits the Label Size

4 min read · Buying Guide

You order a "size 10" and it comes up like a size 6, or a "medium" that fits like a large. If this keeps happening to you on secondhand purchases, it's not bad luck — sizing on used clothing genuinely does behave differently to buying new, for a few concrete reasons.

Sizing standards change over time

Clothing size charts aren't fixed — brands have quietly adjusted their sizing over the decades, and a "12" from 15-20 years ago is often noticeably smaller than a "12" cut today. This is sometimes called vanity sizing, and it means an older secondhand piece can run smaller than the same label size would suggest if it were bought new today.

Fabric changes shape over time

Natural fibres like cotton and wool can shrink slightly with washing over years of ownership, even with careful care. Stretch fabrics containing elastane lose some of their recovery over time, meaning a stretchy piece may fit looser than it did originally, while non-stretch pieces may have tightened slightly through shrinkage.

Brand sizing was never standardised to begin with

Unlike shoe sizing, which follows recognised UK/EU/US systems, clothing sizing has no single enforced standard across brands even today. A "medium" varies significantly between a fast-fashion brand and a premium tailoring brand — buying secondhand just adds a time dimension to a problem that already existed.

How to buy secondhand without the guesswork

  • Always check actual measurements, not just the label size. Chest, waist, length in centimetres or inches tells you far more than "M" or "12" ever will.
  • Compare to something you already own. Lay a similar item you own flat and compare pit-to-pit or waist measurements directly against the listing's numbers.
  • Ask the seller for exact measurements if they're not listed. Any seller confident in what they're selling should be able to grab a tape measure in two minutes.
  • Factor in the era, if known. Genuinely vintage pieces (think decades old) are more likely to run smaller than their label suggests than something from the last five to ten years.
